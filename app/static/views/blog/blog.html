Me

Hi world! My name is Forrest. I'm a 25 year old guy from the US, leaving behind a high-paying job at a hedge fund for long-term, low-budget travel around the world. Here's the story of how I got here.


I grew up in Kansas. Maybe it's because there wasn't much to do there and I had to make my own entertainment, maybe it's just who I am, but I developed a taste for exploration and adventure. My friends and I would ride our bikes around when we were younger, checking out new neighborhoods, occasionally getting into trouble for our shenanigans. My adventure-seeking continued to grow with time. I picked up pole vaulting in high school, which was a great outlet for my energy. In college, I joined an awesome group of guys in a fraternity. We drank a lot, did secret fraternity stuff, the whole deal. I studied neuroscience in school, and spent as much time experimenting with substances outside of class as I did in my labs. An amazing, fun, transformative, and surreal four years later, I graduated and was ready to move on.


And so I entered the "real world," a bright-eyed kid armed with a strong sense of optimism and a newly acquired degree. Instead of following the traditional neuroscience path of becoming a doctor or doing research, I decided this was my chance to try out a job, make some money, and see what else was out there. I was really lucky and managed to get a job at Goldman Sachs, so I was off to NYC! Say what you want about the vampire squids, but I have a ton of respect for Goldman. I was surrounded by talented, driven people, and they taught me how to really work. At the same time, I got to live in one of the most amazing cities I've ever been to (more on that later)! I had a great group of friends, enough money for the expensive lifestyle, and the youthful endurance needed to go out in the city that never sleeps.


A fun and intense year later, I again got lucky and was offered a job at a quantitative hedge fund in Connecticut. I accepted, jumping at the chance to learn from former Harvard professors and some of the most respected people in the quant world. But as I settled in, I realized I wasn't happy. Yes, I was making more money than a 23 year old probably should and was living what looked like a nice life. Black cars drove me from Connecticut back to NYC where I still lived. I could (and am embarrassed to say, did) spend hundreds of dollars a night on dinner or going out. But I wasn't happy. Life wasn't about making money so I could buy nice things, then needing to make even more money to buy even nicer things. I wanted to live an inspired life filled with amazing people and experiences and help others do the same. I wanted to surround myself with passionate, inspired people who were living the dream!


So here I am. Leaving behind the safety of a good, stable job for the adventure of the road. Working to create something I believe in, to help others see the world that I'm lucky enough to experience.



Living Homelessly

At the end of January I got rid of my apartment, packed everything I needed into my backpack, and started living homelessly. It's been a great month, and especially great as my last month in the country! I've been able to see good friends one last time, save some extra money for my travels, and also test out my travel gear. I definitely recommend it for those who are planning a nomadic lifestlye.


Still, it's not for everyone. I remember my first day of being homeless - I got back to the city around 6pm, the sun was going down, and I felt nervous that I might not find a place to sleep for the night. Luckily, my cousin James was around. We grabbed a drink, hung out at the bar for awhile, and I ended up crashing on his couch. It turned out to be a really fun night! Since then, I haven't felt that same twinge of nervousness. Instead, I've felt empowered, free to explore and move about. I've stayed with friends in New York, Connecticut, and even Colorado. With no worries of where I need to end up for the night, I've been able to be much more spontaneous.


I've had great nights that started with no plans. One night I ended up going out with this cool girl I first met on Tinder, meeting up with my friend from school, and getting brunch the next morning before work (weekday brunch is awesome by the way)! I've also had nights that went less smoothly. Another night I was at my friend's party, having a great time. We decided to go out to the lower east side and bust out our sweet dance moves. We got separated during the night, and I ended up not being able to stay at her place like I'd thought I would. I had this realization at around 3am. It also happened to be one of the coldest nights of the year! Luckily, another friend lived around the area, and I was able to sleep at his place. All in all, it's been a great experience. Even the unexpected, tough times have ended up as good memories.


Don't get me wrong, it's definitely nice to have a home. It's comfortable and stable, a great place to come back to and relax. You don't worry about where you're going to sleep, or shower, or go to the bathroom (turns out a gym membership is super useful, at least for the latter two). You don't worry about how you're going to fit a months worth of work clothes into a backpack. But at the same time, an apartment can become burdensome. It's been very freeing to finally remove the clutter from my life. My apartment was full of stuff I didn't need, but that I held onto, for some distant day in the future. I think it was mostly a fear of letting go. It's been nice to let go of that stuff, and then of my apartment too. Tomorrow, I'll be fully uncluttering my life by letting go of one last thing - my job.



Goodbye Corporate Life

My last corporate email:


All,


Today is my last day at AQR. Even though this wasn’t the place for me, I’m thankful for my time here and for everything I’ve learned. I now have a better understanding of finance, of the “real world,” and more importantly, of myself. I’ve learned to be more practical, while realizing that I’m a deeply idealistic person. I’ve learned to appreciate the structure and routine of daily corporate life, while realizing that I thrive in a dynamic, flexible environment. Ultimately, I’ve learned that staying here would mean not staying true to myself.


That’s too bad, since staying here would put me on a very nice, comfortable path. Year after year, we make more money. We eat out at ever-fancier restaurants, then come back to our progressively nicer houses. We take our families on ever-more-luxurious vacations. But this isn’t the life I dream of. Sure, it sounds like a good life. I can’t argue with that. It turns out that $400 meals can be pretty tasty. And it’s almost ridiculous to not be upset after waking up to a $600 bill from the night before (thanks again for splitting that guys). But still, I feel a lack of fulfillment.


What’s important to me is to have a purpose, to fight for something meaningful. Yes, maybe our purpose is to help participants in underfunded pensions receive the benefits they’ve been promised. But if I really believed in that, I would work for less money. Instead, over the past few years I’ve become more and more obsessed with making money, in part because I’ve used it as a measure of myself. I worry that I’ve lost the sense of purpose that I once felt, the desire to help the world in some meaningful way. Of course, I say all of this from a position of privilege, while making enough to not be worried about money. But now that I’m here, this is an opportunity I can’t waste.


So I’m making a change. Wiping away this clear path toward “success” was tough. The road ahead of me is less clear. But too many people avoid taking the first step forward, just because they can’t see where the path leads. I don’t want to live my life like that. I’m off in pursuit of something more.


I’ll be traveling the world, heading out on a one way flight to Thailand on March 11. Not sure how long I’ll be abroad – I’m thinking a year or so to start. At the same time, I’ll be working on my own project. You can follow my progress and travel experiences here: http://www.splashee.com/#/blog. Hope to see some of you out on the road.


- Forrest

Hopes/Dreams/Fears


What do I hope to accomplish over this next period of my life? I hope to see the world and the amazing people and experiences it's filled with. To understand different perspectives and ways of life. To be amazed and shocked, to question what I know and to grow from that. I want to put myself in new, untested environments. To gauge my current views against what are now only unknown unknowns. I imagine there will be plenty of "what the fuck!?" moments - both good and bad. Though there will be locals, backpackers, and other people around me, I imagine my true company will be myself. It'll be a time to reflect, to enjoy my own company and better understand who I am and what I want. I have a certain set of ideas now, and we'll see how these hold up. No matter what, I'll treasure this time and opportunity that I have. But I do fear some consequences. Not of disease, injury, and crime, though those are real issues. I fear that this trip will take me off the deep end. That by becoming some blend of all these different worlds, I'll never be able to integrate into any individual one. That I'll be so changed by the experience that I wont ever accept a more "normal" life. I know already that's a part of my work attitude. I'm constantly reaching, working to create and improve. At first I worried that I may not ever truly be happy if I'm never satisfied with the status quo. Over time, I've learned to enjoy the process of constantly reaching, and not the accomplishment of reaching some given target. I think this could also be a dangerous attiude for relationships. So far it hasn't been the same, but then again, I've lived and enjoyed the single life for quite awhile, so maybe something's thre.

Bangkok - First Day


After what felt like a surprisingly short flight from NYC to Bangkok (layover in Stockholm), I landed in Bangkok! First things first - I walked around the airport looking for food to refuel before trying to find my hostel. It took me a couple tries to follow the signs to the food court; apparently you're supposed to walk through the bathrooms to find the food court on the other side. I got 35 baht worth of food tickets (~33 baht = 1 USD), and used those to buy a water and some stir fried veggies over rice. At the sauce station, I decided to be bold and ladle some of this mystery brown sauce over part of my rice. Not my best move. It ended up being this kind of heavy, stinky, sweet-sour souce. Oh well, live and learn. The natural flavor of the stir friend veggies was pretty good, so all in all still a solid meal. At this point, I figured I now had ~1 hour timeframe to get to my hostel before the diarrhea hit :O ! Gotta perform under pressure. Luckily, hostelworld had really straightforward directions, and it was a nice trip over to the Saphaipae Hostel. Also, no diarrhea! Maybe the airport food was just really clean, maybe the boiling hot water you dip the utensils in at the utensil station actually killed some germs.


I made my way up to my room, which was a 10 bed mixed room. Nice, clean quarters, with air conditioning too, all for 10 USD/night. There are two dudes and a girl in there when I arrive, and we start chatting. We talk about Bangkok, partying, the Bali Nine, relationships on the road, a whole bunch of stuff. I find the dynamic of dating while both people are traveling really interesting. One of the guys has been traveling since 2011, and has recently been dating a Finnish girl. They're going through a rough patch and she's out of the country, but apparently they have a pretty sweet apartment in downtown Bangkok so who knows where that's going.


We get to talking about activities for the day. It turns out the girl is leaving the hostel today, and she gives me a couple thousand Cambodian riel (in case I end up going there) before she heads out. It converts to less than 1 USD, but maybe good for a meal or something. One of the guys also heads out, after deciding to climb up this 49-story abandoned "ghost tower". It looked pretty sweet, but I wanted to explore the area some more first, so I decided to give myself a walking tour around the Bang Rak district where my hostel is. I wandered away from the more commercial/touristy areas, into the alleys that were lined with little stands. The atmospere reminded me a lot of China, maybe Shanghai, but cleaner, more organized, and a bit quieter. All the delicious smells from the street foods were calling to me, so I stopped for another small meal. I went for some mystery street meat, with some kind of leafy green vegetable, over rice. The meat was nice, savory, and stew-y, with some bitterness from the veggies. Pretty flavorful and overall, nice solid meal. I was more worried about how my stomach would handle this meal from a random street stand, but still, the iron stomach held strong :) .


After wandering what felt like a few miles in each direction, I got lost. It was a nice chance to talk to some locals and try to figure out directions (backup pre-downloaded map on my phone, and also a map from the hostel, but I'd rather ask someone). I talked to a few people who looked around my age, and they all spoke decent English. Apparently not good enough, because the last kid I asked directions from told me the opposite way, and so I ended up accidentally crossing the Chao Phraya River. No biggie, there was a nice view from the bridge, I got some extra exercise, and saw more of the city. I also got pretty good at dodging cars, since there were almost no stop lights in the area, and crossing the street was like a real life game of frogger.


Bangkok - Day Two Highlights


Lunch

Had some good food and nice conversation at lunch. I was on my way to check out the Bangkok Arts and Culture Center, got hungry, so started scouting out restaurants. Finally, I decided to go into this small-ish place that was pretty filled with local people. I sat down and pointed to a picture of what looked like flat noodles and meat. The owner warned me that it was spicy, I told him I can handle spicy, and we started talking. Turns out he's wasn't Thai, he was actually Chinese (third generation in Thailand). As I found out later, Chinese people seem to integrate pretty well into Thailand. I guess he'd integrated a bit too well and didn't speak Mandarin, and since my Thai-speaking abilities were non-existent so far, we stumbled along in English. I capped off that meal with some interesting dessert, which he explained was some red, gel-like water-chestnut, relatively rare, thick-skinned coconut (1 of these for 4 regular coconuts), in coconut milk/syrup.

Bangkok Arts and Culture Center

Armed with my pre-loaded route on Google Maps, I walked around looking for the Bangkok Arts and Culture Center (BACC). As I mentioned above, I took a quick detour for lunch. On the way, I also discovered this impromptu live music played by overall-wearing high-schoolers at this mall-ish place called I'm Park. Stores at the mall included Domino's, a math workout center, and a Botox clinic. I continued on, by the National Stadium (host of the FIFA 2015 World Deaf Futsal Championships) and finally to the BACC.

Asiatique with Gift

I met up this evening with Gift, a friend from Wash U. She's been back in Thailand for about a year now. got good spicy papaya salad, some thai sausage (fermented and regular). Talked about buddhism, yoga, superstition, Thai concepts of time, Thai relationships, college, self-medication, etc.

Thoughts

Current Theories


My price level adapts. Baseline is reasonable, going for good value. Might seem silly to distinguish between 30 baht vs 60 baht, when difference is only $1. I instinctively do though.

Inherently I feel less satisfied with doing nothing/status quo than others appear to feel. Maybe my Maslow hierarchy is better met?

I think I can not give enough importance to other people's feelings.

Ideal pursuit of mission involves some balance of playfulness and urgency.

Looking at this now, most backpacking seems really empty. Just people going experience- gathering. What really matters is how you affect other people. Creating something beautiful and inspiring. For travel, it may be sharing your own culture with theirs. Maybe that's the beauty in backpacking.

Finance is too focused on oneself. Deeper satisfaction comes from making others happy.

Backpacking can both make you more tolerant and less tolerant. Greater tolerance/patience comes as you're exposed to new cultures and see more. But because of the freedom of backpacking, one can also choose to remove himself from situations he doesn't like. That can make you very intolerant of other things (like people in high finance). Be careful of this.

Creating your own meaning. Like the Little Prince Quote - You are beautiful, but you are empty. One cannot die for you.

More important than just traveling for a year, is to have this year to myself, to just explore myself. This time is invaluable. I haven't thought thoughts like these in awhile, and it's only the first day.
