The Beginning

Hi world! My name is Forrest. I'm a 25 year old guy from the US, leaving behind a high-paying job at a hedge fund for long-term, low-budget travel around the world. Here's the story of how I got here.


I grew up in Kansas. Maybe it's because there wasn't much to do there and I had to make my own entertainment, maybe it's just who I am, but I developed a taste for exploration and adventure. My friends and I would ride our bikes around, checking out new neighborhoods, occasionally getting into trouble for our shenanigans. My adventure-seeking continued to grow with time. I picked up pole vaulting in high school, which was a great outlet for my energy. In college, I joined an awesome group of guys in a fraternity. We drank a lot, did secret fraternity stuff, the whole deal. I studied neuroscience in school, and ended up spending as much time experimenting with substances outside of class as I did in the labs. An amazing, fun, transformative, and surreal four years later, I graduated and was ready to move on.


And so I entered the "real world," a bright-eyed kid armed with a strong sense of optimism and a newly acquired degree. Instead of following the traditional neuroscience path of becoming a doctor or doing research, I decided this was my chance to try out a job, make some money, and see what else was out there. I was really lucky and managed to get a job at Goldman Sachs, so I was off to NYC! Say what you want about the vampire squids, but I have a ton of respect for Goldman. I was surrounded by talented, driven people, and they taught me how to really work. At the same time, I got to live in one of the most amazing cities I've ever been to (more on that later)! I had great friends, enough money for the expensive lifestyle, and the youthful endurance needed to go out in the city that never sleeps.


An fun and intense year later, I again got lucky and was offered a job at a quantitative hedge fund in Connecticut. I accepted, jumping at the chance to learn from former Harvard professors and some of the most respected people in the quant world. But as I settled in, I realized I wasn't happy. Yes, I was making more money than a 23 year old probably should and was living what looked like a nice life. Black cars drove me from Connecticut back to NYC where I still lived. I could (and am embarrassed to say, sometimes did) spend over $500 a night on dinner or going out. But I wasn't happy. Life wasn't about making money so I could buy nice things, then needing to make even more money to buy even nicer things. I wanted to live an inspired life filled with amazing people and experiences and help others do the same. I wanted to surround myself with passionate, inspired people who were living the dream!


So here I am. Leaving behind the safety of a good, stable job for the adventure of the road. Working to create something I believe in, to help others see the world that I'm lucky enough to experience.



Living Homelessly

At the end of January I got rid of my apartment, packed everything I needed into my backpack, and started living homelessly. It's been a great month, and especially great as my last month in the country! I've been able to see good friends one last time, save some extra money for my travels, and also test out my travel gear. I definitely recommend it for those who are planning a nomadic lifestlye.


Still, it's not for everyone. I remember my first day of being homeless - I got back to the city around 6pm, the sun was going down, and I felt nervous that I might not find a place to sleep for the night. Luckily, my cousin James was around. We grabbed a drink, hung out at the bar for awhile, and I ended up crashing on his couch. It turned out to be a really fun night! Since then, I haven't felt that same twinge of nervousness. Instead, I've felt empowered, free to explore and move about. I've stayed with friends in New York, Connecticut, and even Colorado. With no worries of where I need to end up for the night, I've been able to be much more spontaneous.


I've had great nights that started with no plans. One night I ended up going out with this cool girl I first met on Tinder, meeting up with my friend from school, and getting brunch the next morning before work (weekday brunch is awesome by the way)! I've also had nights that went less smoothly. Another night I was at my friend's party, having a great time. We decided to go out to the lower east side and bust out our sweet dance moves. We got separated during the night, and I ended up not being able to stay at her place like I'd thought I would. I had this realization at around 3am. It also happened to be one of the coldest nights of the winter! Luckily, another friend lived around the area, and I was able to sleep at his place. All in all, it's been a great experience. Even the unexpected, tough times have ended up as good memories.


Don't get me wrong, it's definitely nice to have a home. It's comfortable and stable, a great place to come back to and relax. You don't have to worry about where you're going to sleep, or shower, or go to the bathroom (turns out a gym membership is super useful, at least for the latter two). You don't worry about how you're going to fit a months worth of work clothes into a backpack (wearing a fleece over a t-shirt does the trick. Oh yeah, and a pair of pants too if you don't want to get fired). But at the same time, an apartment can become burdensome. It's been very freeing to finally remove the clutter from my life. My apartment was full of stuff I didn't need, but that I held onto, for some distant day in the future. I think it was mostly a fear of letting go. It's been nice to let go of that stuff, and then of my apartment too. Tomorrow, I'll be fully uncluttering my life by letting go of one last thing - my job.
