About Me

Hi world! My name is Forrest. I'm a 25 year old guy from the US, leaving behind a high-paying job at a hedge fund for long-term, low-budget travel around the world. Here's the story of how I got here.


I grew up in Kansas. Maybe it's because there wasn't much to do there and I had to make my own entertainment, maybe it's just who I am, but I developed a taste for exploration and adventure. My friends and I would ride our bikes around when we were younger, checking out new neighborhoods, occasionally getting into trouble for our shenanigans. My adventure-seeking grew with time. I picked up pole vaulting in high school, which was a great outlet for my energy. In college, I joined an awesome group of guys in a fraternity. We drank a lot, did secret fraternity stuff, the whole deal. I studied neuroscience in school, and spent as much time experimenting with substances outside of class as I did in my labs. An amazing, fun, transformative, and surreal four years later, I graduated and was ready to move on.


And so I entered the "real world," a bright-eyed kid armed with a strong sense of optimism and a newly acquired degree. Instead of following the traditional neuroscience path of becoming a doctor or doing research, I decided this was my chance to try out a job, make some money, and see what else was out there. I was really lucky and managed to get a job at Goldman Sachs, so I was off to NYC! Say what you want about the vampire squids, but I have a ton of respect for Goldman. I was surrounded by talented, driven people, and they taught me how to really work. At the same time, I got to live in an amazing city! I had a great group of friends, enough money for the expensive lifestyle, and the youthful endurance needed to go out in the city that never sleeps.


A fun and intense year later, I again got lucky and was offered a job at a quantitative hedge fund in Connecticut. I accepted, jumping at the chance to learn from former Harvard professors and some of the most respected people in the quant world. But as I settled in, I realized I wasn't happy. Yes, I was making more money than a 23 year old probably should and was living what looked like a nice life. Black cars drove me from Connecticut back to NYC where I still lived. I could (and am embarrassed to say, did) spend hundreds of dollars a night on dinner or going out. But I wasn't happy. Life wasn't about making money so I could buy nice things, then needing to make even more money to buy even nicer things. I wanted to live an inspired life filled with amazing people and experiences and help others do the same. I wanted to surround myself with passionate, inspired people who were living the dream!


So here I am. Leaving behind the safety of a good, stable job for the adventure of the road. Working to create something I believe in, to help others see the world that I'm lucky enough to experience.



Living Homelessly

At the end of January (2015) I got rid of my apartment, packed everything I needed into my backpack, and started living homelessly. It's been a great month, and especially great as my last month in the country! I've been able to see good friends one last time, save some extra money for my travels, and also test out my travel gear. I definitely recommend it for those who are planning a nomadic lifestlye.


Still, it's not for everyone. I remember my first day of being homeless - I got back to the city around 6pm, the sun was going down, and I felt nervous that I might not find a place to sleep for the night. Luckily, my cousin James was around. We grabbed a drink, hung out at the bar for awhile, and I ended up crashing on his couch. It turned out to be a really fun night. Since then, I haven't felt that same twinge of nervousness. Instead, I've felt empowered, free to explore and move about. I've stayed with friends in New York, Connecticut, and even Colorado. With no worries of where I need to end up for the night, I've been able to be much more spontaneous.


I've had great nights that started with no plans. One night I ended up going out with this cool girl from Tinder, meeting up with my friend from school, and getting brunch the next morning before work (weekday brunch is awesome by the way)! I've also had nights that went less smoothly. Another night I was at my friend's party, having a great time. We decided to go out to the lower east side and bust out our sweet dance moves. We got separated during the night, and I ended up not being able to stay at her place like I'd thought I would. I had this realization at around 3am. It also happened to be one of the coldest nights of the year! Luckily, another friend lived around the area, and I was able to sleep at his place. All in all, it's been a great experience. Even the unexpected, tough times have ended up as good memories.


Don't get me wrong, it's definitely nice to have a home. It's comfortable and stable, a great place to come back to and relax. You don't worry about where you're going to sleep, or shower, or go to the bathroom (turns out a gym membership is super useful, at least for the latter two). You don't worry about how you're going to fit a months worth of work clothes into a backpack. But at the same time, an apartment can become burdensome. It's been very freeing to finally remove the clutter from my life. My apartment was full of stuff I didn't need, but that I held onto, for some distant day in the future. I think it was mostly a fear of letting go. It's been nice to let go of that stuff, and then of my apartment too. Tomorrow, I'll be fully uncluttering my life by letting go of one last thing - my job.



Goodbye Corporate Life

My last corporate email:


All,


Today is my last day at AQR. Even though this wasn’t the place for me, I’m thankful for my time here and for everything I’ve learned. I now have a better understanding of finance, of the “real world,” and more importantly, of myself. I’ve learned to be more practical, while realizing that I’m a deeply idealistic person. I’ve learned to appreciate the structure and routine of daily corporate life, while realizing that I thrive in a dynamic, flexible environment. Ultimately, I’ve learned that staying here would mean not staying true to myself.


That’s too bad, since staying here would put me on a very nice, comfortable path. Year after year, we make more money. We eat out at ever-fancier restaurants, then come back to our progressively nicer houses. We take our families on ever-more-luxurious vacations. But this isn’t the life I dream of. Sure, it sounds like a good life. I can’t argue with that. It turns out that $400 meals can be pretty tasty. And it’s almost ridiculous to not be upset after waking up to a $600 bill from the night before (thanks again for splitting that guys). But still, I feel a lack of fulfillment.


What’s important to me is to have a purpose, to fight for something meaningful. Yes, maybe our purpose is to help participants in underfunded pensions receive the benefits they’ve been promised. But if I really believed in that, I would work for less money. Instead, over the past few years I’ve become more and more obsessed with making money, in part because I’ve used it as a measure of myself. I worry that I’ve lost the sense of purpose that I once felt, the desire to help the world in some meaningful way. Of course, I say all of this from a position of privilege, while making enough to not be worried about money. But now that I’m here, this is an opportunity I can’t waste.


So I’m making a change. Wiping away this clear path toward “success” was tough. The road ahead of me is less clear. But too many people avoid taking the first step forward, just because they can’t see where the path leads. I don’t want to live my life like that. I’m off in pursuit of something more.


I’ll be traveling the world, heading out on a one way flight to Thailand on March 11. Not sure how long I’ll be abroad – I’m thinking a year or so to start. At the same time, I’ll be working on my own project. You can follow my progress and travel experiences here: http://www.splashee.com/#/blog. Hope to see some of you out on the road.


- Forrest

Hopes/Dreams/Fears


What do I hope to accomplish over this next period of my life? I hope to see the world and the amazing people and experiences it's filled with. To understand different perspectives and ways of life. To be amazed and shocked, to question what I know and to grow from that. I want to put myself in new, untested environments. To gauge my current views against what are now only unknown unknowns. I imagine there will be plenty of "what the fuck!?" moments - both good and bad. Though there will be locals, backpackers, and other people around me, I imagine my true company will be myself. It'll be a time to reflect, to enjoy my own company and better understand who I am and what I want. I have a certain set of ideas now, and we'll see how these hold up. No matter what, I'll treasure this time and opportunity that I have. But I do fear some consequences. Not of disease, injury, and crime, though those are real issues. I fear that this trip will take me off the deep end. That by becoming some blend of all these different worlds, I'll never be able to integrate into any individual one. That I'll be so changed by the experience that I wont ever accept a more "normal" life. I know already that's a part of my work attitude. I'm constantly reaching, working to create and improve. At first I worried that I may not ever truly be happy if I'm never satisfied with the status quo. Over time, I've learned to enjoy the process of constantly reaching, and not the accomplishment of reaching some given target. I think this could also be a dangerous attiude for relationships. So far it hasn't been the same, but then again, I've lived and enjoyed the single life for quite awhile, so maybe something's thre.

We created universities to with different types of standard degrees, taught people in a mostly-standardized lecture format, and cranked out degrees. First Stop - Bangkok!

After what felt like a surprisingly short flight from NYC to Bangkok (layover in Stockholm), I landed in Bangkok! First things first - I walked around the airport looking for food to refuel before trying to find my hostel. It took me a couple tries to follow the signs to the food court; apparently you're supposed to walk through the bathrooms to find the food court on the other side. I got 35 baht worth of food tickets (~33 baht = 1 USD), and used those to buy a water and some stir fried veggies over rice. At the sauce station, I decided to be bold and ladle some of this mystery brown sauce over part of my rice. Not my best move. It ended up being this kind of heavy, stinky, sweet-sour sauce. Oh well, live and learn. The natural flavor of the stir friend veggies was pretty good, so all in all still a solid meal. At this point, I figured I now had ~1 hour timeframe to get to my hostel before the diarrhea hit :O ! Gotta perform under pressure. Luckily, Hostelworld had really straightforward directions, and it was a nice trip over to the Saphaipae Hostel. Also, no diarrhea! Maybe the airport food was just really clean, maybe the boiling hot water for dipping the utensils in at the utensil station actually killed some germs.


I made my way up to my room, which was a 10 bed mixed room. Nice, clean quarters, with air conditioning too, all for 10 USD/night. There are two dudes and a girl in there when I arrive, and we start chatting. We talk about Bangkok, partying, the Bali Nine, relationships on the road, a whole bunch of stuff. I find the dynamic of dating while both people are traveling really interesting. One of the guys has been traveling since 2011, and has recently been dating a Finnish girl. They're going through a rough patch and she's out of the country, but apparently they have a pretty sweet apartment in downtown Bangkok so who knows where that's going.


We get to talking about activities for the day. It turns out the girl is leaving the hostel today, and she gives me a couple thousand Cambodian riel (in case I end up going there) before she heads out. It converts to less than 1 USD, but maybe good for a meal or something. One of the guys also heads out, after deciding to climb up this 49-story abandoned "ghost tower". It looked pretty sweet, but I wanted to explore the area some more first, so I decided to give myself a walking tour around the Bang Rak district where my hostel is. I wandered away from the more commercial/touristy areas, into the alleys that were lined with little stands. The atmospere reminded me a lot of China, maybe Shanghai, but cleaner, more organized, and a bit quieter. All the delicious smells from the street foods were calling to me, so I stopped for another small meal. I went for some mystery street meat, with some kind of leafy green vegetable, over rice. The meat was nice, savory, and stew-y, with some bitterness from the veggies. Pretty flavorful and overall, nice solid meal. I was more worried about how my stomach would handle this meal from a random street stand, but still, the iron stomach held strong :) .


After wandering what felt like a few miles in each direction, I got lost. It was a nice chance to talk to some locals and try to figure out directions (backup pre-downloaded map on my phone, and also a map from the hostel, but I'd rather ask someone). I talked to a few people who looked around my age, and they all spoke decent English. Though apparently not good enough, because the last kid I asked directions from told me the opposite way, and so I ended up accidentally crossing the Chao Phraya River. No biggie, there was a nice view from the bridge, I got some extra exercise, and saw more of the city. I also got pretty good at dodging cars, since there were almost no stop lights in the area and I had to cross the streets somehow.

Asiatique with Gift

I met up this evening with Gift, a friend from Wash U. She's been back in Thailand for about a year now. We decided to take a boat over to Asiatique, this kind of outdoor mall/market/carnival thing. Like Chinese culture, food seems to be a huge part of Thai culture. We walked around to all the little stands and Gift pointed out some foods I should try. We ended up with a bunch of little dishes - some spicy papaya salad, thai sausage (fermented and regular), squid stuffed with egg, salmon. It was fun to try out a little of everything. Like when a bus is leaving at 8am, it actually leaves when it fills up with people. Thai people seem much more relaxed about when things occur, and people don't really seem to be in too much of a rush. Generally pretty chill. There also seems to be a religious/superstitious tint over a lot of things. I definitely want to learn more about Buddhism, it seems like it's got some sweet ideas.



Visit From Andrew

My friend Andrew (a trader on Wall Street) just got a new job, and so was out traveling for a few weeks. He'd been around Thailand and Australia (which he loved), and was stopping by Bangkok for a few hours before his flight back to NYC. We met up at the Phaya Thai metro station and walked around in search of food. We eventually settled on a western place, grabbed some drinks and a sandwich, and chatted for awhile.


Andrew's tour sounded pretty cool. Parts were adventurous (maybe a bit too adventurous for some - an older couple had ended up in the hospital after some boating mishaps), parts were domestic (learning to cook Thai food), and overall seemed like a nice, balanced tour that gave them a flavor of local life. I'll learn some things from that organization.


Most of the people on his tour were older, and some were retired and traveling around the world. I couldn't easily find age distributions for around-the-world travelers, but based on my experience I'd guess that most tend to be the young (<25) or the old (>65, or retired). For some reason there's some period during the bulk of people's lives when they just don't travel. Maybe it's due to the stability requirements of having children, maybe it's a societal thing. It reminds me of this Japanese dude in my hostel, who was on a final month-long trip around Asia before starting his job. As he said, this was his last chance to travel, and then his life was over. The sad thing about it is that he didn't say it in that half-joking way that some Americans might have. He said it like he truly believed that after this trip, he had a lifetime of work ahead of him with no end in sight but death (which as he told me was how it was). Bummer.


Ok sorry, back to lunch. As Andrew observed, NYC has the best of a lot of things. He expected Thai food here to be very different or in some way better than Thai food in New York, but was disappointed. Similar taste, lower sanitation standard, slow service, etc. While I tend not to think of things in terms of better or worse, I think many people from the US would agree with him. And this reminds me that I think I have a different utility function than many people. I appreciate that the service is slow - it reminds me that there's a very different concept of time than the one I'm used to experiencing. I don't like the lower sanitation standard, but like the reminder that people are used to living in very different conditions than the sheltered world I come from.

Meditation Retreat

As I mentioned before, I was talking to a friend from school (Gift) in Bangkok and got interested in meditation. I applied for a spot in a 10-day meditation retreat (website here) and was accepted, so I hopped on a flight to Johor Bahru, Malaysia. Here are my thoughts.


Wow. 10 days, 10 hours of meditation a day. No contact with the outside world. No speaking, eye contact, or gestures with other students. No reading or writing material allowed. It was a long time to spend with my own thoughts, but ultimately a great experience and I'm thankful to get this experience at the start of my trip!


Ok - get ready for a wall of text. A few pictures to help you get through it all (taken at the end of the retreat - no phone allowed during). I've also sprinkled in a couple of pictures my fellow meditators sent me throughout the text, so look forward to those!



Overall, I think Vipassana meditation has a really nice philosophy. A lot of things I agree with, a couple things I dont. Oh yeah, keep in mind everything that follows is my interpretation of what I experienced during the course. I'll do more outside research over time, and welcome comments/critiques on things I may have misunderstood.


The core idea was that unhappiness is not external, but is instead created by the individual. They told us a story I thought illustrated this nicely. There was a great painter, who painted a beautiful, captivating, amazing portrait of a woman. He looked at the painting and could not live without it - he was in love with this painting, wanted bring it home, lock himself away and just be with the painting forever. People said, "Are you mad? This is a painting, you drew it yourself!" A second painter paints the same woman, but paints a horrifying, ugly picture of the woman. He's disgusted and terrified by his painting and wants to run away from it and hide. Again, people question whether the man is mad - after all, he painted this image himself! Finally, there are two ordinary men, gazing at a woman before them. The first man can't live without her, wants to run away with her and be with her forever. The second man is disgusted and terrified by the woman. Are these two men not mad in the same way the painters are mad? They've painted different pictures of the same woman, and each reacts to his own image. Vipassana meditation tries to make us aware that we are all painters, and have some control over the world we create.


Ok, enough theory. I love that Vipassana is grounded in practice, individual experience, self-observation. So let's get into what actually happened. I land in Johor Bahru and make my way over to the meetup point, funnily enough in front of a KFC at the side of the road. Me and a British dude (Dannie) get picked up by a local girl (Natasha), her mom and her aunt. It's Natasha's first time doing this as well, and her aunt tells us a bit about "aches and pains" halfway through the course, and then the good stuff that happens once you're past that. It was all very vague-sounding, and so we headed into the course with barely any idea what to expect. I sign in and settled into our quarters, ready to see what happens over the next week and a half.


The next morning I'm woken up at 4am by a gong. Off to the meditation hall! The first day we spent entirely focused on breathing. I hadn't really meditated before (besides a training session at Goldman) and found it tough to focus my mind for hours at a time. My mind would wander off in seemingly random directions, and eventually find its way back to my breath. At first I had to breathe heavily, controlling my breath, just to notice it. It was actually pretty tough to only observe my breath without changing it. It felt a bit like trying to lucid dream, when as soon as I become aware I'm dreaming that shocks me into waking up. Over the hours, I was slowly able to fall into natural breathing and as the instructors said, to "just observe", my breath. I was surprised at how light ny natural breathing was. By the end of the day I was constantly observing my breath, even when not meditating. Or maybe just constantly meditating.


The second day echoed the first. Another 10 hours focused on my breath. Since I hadn't read up on Vipassana before the course, and since the course hadn't yet explained the process, I was pretty confused over what we were supposed to be experiencing. I subtly felt that my mind was calmer and more stable, with fewer bouts of wandering off in random directions. I wondered if meditation might actually decrease creativity because of this. Intinctively, I think not.


Despite feeling like time passed relatively quickly while meditating, I began to look forward to events that broke the monotony. Our 6am and 11am meals were really nice. Usually some rice, a few vegetarian dishes, and soup. Plus some dessert or fruit. I usually don't like what I call fake meat, but theirs was actually really good with very meaty texture. My favorite time of day was early evening tea. They would brew this "cooling herbal tea." Really soothing, purplish tea, a bit sweet and earthy tasting. I would grab a piece of fruit, often guava with maple sugar, and walk around the courtyard. My favorite tree was this one with spiky leaves. There was also a little pond that I noticed people kept going to. I was curious, wandered over, and saw this cool little terrapin in it.


Day 3 - We finally moved on from focusing on breathing! We moved attention to feeling sensations around the nose and area above the upper lip, and then to only sensations on the upper lip. Good thing I've been growing this mustache! The easiest things to feel were wind from breathing, coldness and heat, and little tingles. The reason for focusing on this area is apparently the smaller the area of focus, the sharper the mind becomes. For the next day and a half we focused on this small area.


One thing I haven't mentioned yet is evening discourses. For about an hour a day, we'd watch a video lesson from S.N. Goenka. This would provide more of the theory behind Vapassana, and behind what we were doing for the day. By Day 3, I was starting to get an idea of why we were spending so long focusing on breathing and this small area above our upper lip. People usually have the perspective of looking out at the world, and here we were developing a new perspective of looking inwards. Instead of sensing the external world and reacting unconsciously, we would try to alter our unconscious reactions with better mind-body awareness. I'm not totally sure about the causality but the idea was that perceptions of the outside world create reactions linked to sensations on the body. For instance, you can see how if you are angry at something, your breathing may become heavier, and your body may feel warmer. The clever idea here is that you can train your subconscious mind to become aware of these sensations when you react to something. Then you can draw your conscious awareness to recognize and accept the sensation, and watch it pass, instead of multiplying your reactions through attachment or aversion to the sensation. This can help break habit patterns that cause unhappiness. Whether it's pain or pleasure, heaviness or lightness, heat or cold, tingling, perspiration, numbness, any sensation will arise and pass away. The nature of everything is temporary - anicca.


The end goal is to develop perfect equanimity. You are agnostic to feelings of fear or anger, misery, but surprisingly to me, also feelings of attachment and passion. Apparently since pleasant feelings will eventually go away, you want to get rid of attachment to them.


Day 4 - Today was the big day! This was the day when we were going to move to the heart of Vipassana. Halfway through the day, we moved our attention away from sensations on our upper lip, piece by piece around the rest of our body. This was a huge shock! We started at the tops of our heads, and by the time I got to my neck and shoulders I was feeling a lot of pain. Luckily Natasha's mom had given us some warning of aches and pains. I guess it was no surprise after sitting in meditation for three and a half days, but the sudden sensitivity to the stiffness, the deep soreness, and heaviness in my body was overwhelming. We weren't supposed to move so I couldn't even try to stretch out, and the idea is that sensations arise and pass away, so I bore the pain. It was a rough hour. We took a five minute break and then continued. I was NOT looking forward to the next couple of hours. There's that aversion to sensation that I was supposed to be getting rid of :P.


After our break, I went back and continued moving my attention bit by bit down my body. This time, when I got to my shoulders, something new and really great happened. The sensations of the different areas of my body started blending together, and things started vibrating. As I pushed focus around to other areas, they got merged into this feeling, and soon my whole body was buzzing/humming and it felt like every cell was feeling every sensation at once! It reminded me a bit of the come-up to some of my ecstatic moments. I would later learn that I was experiencing uniform subtle sensations, as opposed to the gross sensations I was feeling earlier. Since I had the impression I had jumped ahead, I spent the next day and a half experimenting with this feeling, pushing it stronger, figuring out how to activate it quickly, shifting the focal point to different areas of the body, etc. This was really great, but apparently I overdid it and grew attached to the uniform suble sensations.


My mind focused on the feeling and would not let it go. The sensation became so strong and persistent that at night I would stay awake for hours because my body was buzzing so hard. By the second night of this, I had gotten to a kind of breaking point. From talking to others after the course, it seemed like this was something a lot of people felt around this time. For some, past health issues came up. One guy had noticed or thought that his breathing had actually stopped. Since he was born with lung issues and had pneumonia or something bad before, he actually got rushed to the hospital late one night, where apparently the doctors said he was fine. I guess when you're doing the deep "mental surgery" that we were doing, as Goenka put it, deep issues come up. Others just couldn't take it and thought about leaving. One of the girls became convinced that this black man was showing up in front of her while she was sleeping.

Bonus pic of us pushing Blank's car out of a ditch. It had gotten stuck that night when he was rushed to the hospital:



For me, I was worried that I was falling into insanity as I tried to be rational. I created this feeling on my own, I thought, and now it's so strong that it keeps me awake. Why can't I will this away? It seemed to me that I had gone so far into my mind that I couldn't pull myself out. Or maybe I had spent so much time focusing on this during the day that my subconscious continued to focus on it, and I had to retrain my subconscious. Reminds me a bit of this Paul Graham article.


But after days of attempting to retrain my focus on my breath, with limited success, I slowly just let it go. And that's what I needed to do from the start, according to the Vipassana technique. I accepted that this is how I was going to feel, and this mental detachment helped stop the magnification/multiplication of my aversion to the feeling. Over the rest of the course, I was able to sleep better.


Though I struggled at night, I enjoyed this same experience during the day. It helped me deal well with the removal of Sankara. The idea is that as we sit, we notice sensations come to the body. We remain equanimous whether they are pleasant or unpleasant sensations, and as the sensations pass we have purified ourselves more. I wasn't the most still meditator, but I could spend an hour at a time during our periods of strong determination sitting completely still. When I chose to. In some cases I just didn't see the benefit of remaining equanimous. Sure, sensations arise and pass. But it's not always best to just observe. Sensations can be signals from your body that you need to take action. During one of my personal sessions with the instructor I asked her (we can break noble silence to ask the instructor questions) about a hypothetical situation where you hit your arm on a table. With an equanimous mindset, you can say ok, I feel this pain, it will arise and pass away. But then maybe you don't go to the doctor, who would tell you your arm is broken and you need to put it in a cast to let it heal properly. Her answer was that in one case you would know because the pain continues, but I wasn't very satisfied with that answer.


Besides focusing on removing Sankara, I also experimented with different sensations that arose as I thought about different things, to get a better idea of what strong attachments I had. Unsurprisingly, attachment to my family was super strong. After days of trying to practice equanimity, my body had a natural tendency to sweep away sensations. But here, I was very resistant to this sweep away, and eventually my body stopped trying. Another strong sensation I felt was the feeling associated with appreciation of the beauty of the world, the magic of living, the overwhelming awe of amazing people and experiences that happen. Interestingly, this was a similar sensation to the one I felt when thinking about my family. I guess I'm really attached to that sensation. I think the desire to share that sensation with others will be part if the mission behind this site.


By Day 8 or 9 I felt I had reached some stable point of equanimity. Not the perfect equanimity of Vapassana but a level I was happy with. It was cool to see a change in others and myself in our daily interactions. Things that we would have gotten upset about at the start, we were now understanding of. I think much had to do with reducing the ego, another form of attachment. I remember getting annoyed at some point when this old dude just jumped in front of me while I was waiting to use the sink to wash my dishes. I realize that much of my angry or annoyed reactions are based in my ego. Putting more importance on myself or what I want than on what others want, which I think is somewhat natural but which I think I could work to reduce. On the other hand there is a need to stand up for oneself when the time arises. Like everything, some balance. Actually one of the main things I think Vapassana does is to try to dissolve the ego. Seems kind of contradictory since it's also focusing on the self.


Day 10 was great. We broke noble silence in the morning, and we finally started connecting with others. We also got three full meals, instead of two meals and tea. It's difficult to appreciate what you have until it's taken away. When you're healthy, you don't appreciate what you have until you're sick. When you can talk you don't appreciate that until it's taken away.

One framework that came out of meditating was categorizing thoughts based on past and future, positive and negative. I found most of my thoughts were focused on the future, and positive. Especially thoughts surrounding career and that aspect of life. With thoughts about people and relationships, most of my thoughts were about the past and positive. I do think I'm lucky in that I'm biased towards positive thoughts and happiness.

Equanimity is good for dealing with anger/annoyment, but I want to experience big highs associated with passion and attachment, and I'm ok with the accompanying big lows.


Actually, I think I get attached to others easily, and want to control this. These attachments conflict with my desire for freedom and independence. I think I often defer to what others want and have a desire to please them. I have strong views of my own too.

Crossroads

After the meditation retreat, we got dropped of at the side of the road next to the main highway. People had plans to head to Kuala Lumpur, Malacca, Singapore, all over. Some were getting picked up, some were taking the bus, some were hitchhiking. I hadn't made any plans for after the retreat, but I had been considering heading down to Singapore, either on a bus with this guy from Barcelona (Mane) or maybe grabbing a ride with someone driving down there. It was a new city and I had a family friend I could stay with, so maybe a cool place to check out. After talking to some of the Singaporeans, I was less psyched to head there. It seemed like a hectic, stressed out place, with a lot of the asian focus on prestige and being the best along a well-defined track. Ok that's probably not a fair representation, but I wasn't feeling it. I ended up talking to a German girl (Miriam) who was headed to this island called Tioman Island. It was a few hours north, and apparently a really beautiful island. I flipped through her Lonely Planet guide, and it sounded awesome - great beaches, jungles, a nice relaxing place to ease back into the real world after 10 days of meditation. So me and this other German girl (Melanie), who was also thinking about heading to Singapore but changed her mind, hopped in a car with Miriam and a fellow meditator and got dropped off at the nearest bus station. At the bus station, I really saw and appreciated the trust travelers/backpackers have with each other. Miriam and Melanie left me - a relative stranger - to watch their stuff while they used the restroom. And I left them to guard our stuff while I scouted for tickets. Very cool. We got lucky and managed to rush onto a bus that was just pulling out of the station, and were on the way to Tioman Island!


First Night in Tioman

After a few hours on the bus and some confusion over the ferry ticketing counters, we were finally on the ferry to Tioman. Melanie was pretty tired and napped for a bit, while me and Miriam chatted. Miriam was on a career break from an intense job that had taken her around Germany, France, and Switzerland. Over the next couple of hours, I got perspective on everything from food differences between the countries to what's it's like being a woman in each place. From worries of future motherhood to gender power dynamics in the workplace. Props to Miriam for learning how to deal with such a male-dominated work environment. At the same time, bummer for having to face that in the first place. I've started to see more awareness of some of the issues women face in the workplace, and I hope that as word spreads, things will get better. Our conversation continued from there, on to personality, self-discovery, girl looks vs guy status, etc. So our ferry ride passed quickly and before we realized, we were on Tioman!


We landed on Tioman Island in the early evening. Miriam had planned a kind of around-the-world meditation/spiritual trip for herself, and so had already booked her hostel. Like me, Melanie is more of an in-the-moment traveler (I would guess a strong "P" in Myers-Briggs tests), and so we ended up on this beautiful island with no place to stay. We started off by walking from the pier to Miriam's place to drop off our backpacks. Melanie had jumped off a really high waterfall a couple months ago and landed wrong, and still had some back pain, so we helped carry some of her stuff. Apparently the first few days after she had mis-landed, she couldn't even move and just had to lay there - pretty scary! It's been getting better slowly.


Miriam's hostel was fully booked, and the area we were staying in didn't have many other hostels, especially not cheaper hostels, so me and Melanie packed some overnight stuff from our main bags and started scouting out places to sleep. Our walk to get dinner took us by a police station. Across from that was this barbed-wire enclosed recreation area where kids were playing this volleyball-like game with their feet. We explored a couple of buildings next to the court and found one with some nice space and also something that looked like a giant pool table inside. It seemed promising, so now that we had a spot it was time for dinner! We stopped by a night market and got some spicy pho-like noodles with veggies and fishballs, and some coconut, corn, and sugar-filled crispy pancakes for dessert.


After dinner we parted ways with Miriam. We walked back to the pier, which we decided could also make a nice sleeping spot. It was still pretty early, maybe 9pm, so we just sat and talked for awhile. I like chatting with travelers, because it feels like conversations are more real. Past a lot of the superficial formalities you would be talking about with other people you'd known for the same amount of time. I'm curious why that's the case. Maybe personality types are more similar, maybe the shared experience of traveling creates an automatic bond, maybe something else.


Melanie was from a small village in Germany (Seeg?), and was the only person who had ever really traveled abroad. So she was seen as the crazy one. From my readings on backpacker studies, a lot of backpackers have advanced education and true to form, she was the only one in her year who had gone onto more advanced studies. We exchanged life/travel stories for awhile, and boy has Melanie has had some big adventures! She's rafted down rivers in Cambodia in a leaky boat she found. She's spent nights in the jungle, with dangerous animals all around and no real protection. She's hitchiked her way across many of the countries by herself. Hearing about all she's done in just half a year has got me pumped for my trip! After a few hours, we head back to our new home. We set up a little spot for ourselves, burned some anti-mosquito incense that another traveler had given Melanie, and called it a night.


The ground was pretty hard, and mosquitos were still biting, but we got a bit of sleep and woke up feeling decently refreshed. We were going to meet up with Miriam for lunch at noon, and had a few hours before that. We decided we wanted to walk over to the next town (~1hr away) to try to find a room at some cheaper hostels. About 10 minutes into our walk, we stopped to ask for directions from this guy at the side of the road. Apparently he ran a small hostel that we hadn't even noticed, and after negotiating the price down by 60%, we decided to get a room there for a couple of days. Now we had a place to stay!


Through the Jungle

After a couple days of relaxing and snorkeling on the beaches of the west coast, I was ready for a change of scenery. We (me, Miriam, Melanie) decided to trek through the jungle to Juara, on the east side of the island. My pumas, banana republic chinos, v-neck t-shirt, and AQR jacket weren't the most optimal for jungle trekking, but I would have to make due. We set out around 9am, after being delayed by a few hours of morning rain.


The jungle we trekked was really nice. Some wildlife, lots of interesting plants, a waterfall, and luckily for us, a path through some parts. Compared to the jungles Melanie had told us about, where they had to cut their own path, this was a breeze! We walked for about an hour and a half and found a little waterfall we had heard about. We dipped our toes in, cooled down, and washed off.


We continued on, and a short while later found a road that cut through the jungle. We walked along for about half an hour, when a nice stranger in a pickup truck stopped by and offered to let us jump in the back. I took this to mean the bed of the truck so hopped in there, while Melanie and Miriam sweet-talked their way to the backseat. It was my first time riding out in the bed of a truck, and I really enjoyed it. We cruised along at a reasonable speed (maybe 40mph) and I got to see some great jungle scenery and take a few truckbed selfies.


Twenty minutes later, we'd arrived to the north end of Juara. We stopped to help the driver unload the truck, then we got changed and walked down the beach. Like the beaches on our side, this beach was nice and peaceful, not loaded up with tourists like I had been partly expecting. It had nice, soft sand, and I liked that the beach curved into a little bay (not sure if that's the right term, hopefully you get what I mean). We stayed there and relaxed for a couple more hours, and Melanie and I decided this would probably be our last night on the island. Miriam wanted to stay on the beach longer and grab a cab back to our side of the island later, so me and Melanie left for the long trek back.


We'd walked for about five minutes when we saw a white car coming down the road. We put out our hands to hitch a ride, and the man and his son were nice enough to pull over and let us in. They didn't speak much English, but I guess we looked pretty harmless, and we understood that they were going back to the west side beach/town (Tekek) also. Half an hour later, we'd made it back! While we were waiting for Miriam to get back to grab our stuff, we found some WiFi and were finally able to check out messages! Sorry it took me so long to get back to some of you - WiFi on the island was not easy to find, and not very reliable!


Miriam finally made it back and we grabbed our bags from her, had a last dinner together, and said goodbye. Melanie and I had decided to catch the 6:30am ferry off the island, so crashed for a few hours at our pool table house (setting up her mosquito net this time!), and then went to the pier. A quick nap on the ferry and a short walk later, Melanie and I finally parted ways at the bus stop. She was going up to Malacca to head over to Indonesia, and I was going to Kuala Lumpur, on my way up to Chiang Mai. We said our goodbyes, and now I'm off to the next set of adventues!


Oh some closing thoughts. This helped me start to appreciate how differently people travel. But also how traveling can bring different people traveling in different ways together. People from all over the world. Melanie taught me how to backpack in a pretty intense way. It's cool because you can travel the world for almost no money and you can stay with locals and really understand different cultures. But of course it involves some risk.

Sharing

In the grown-up (western) world, it seems like we lose the fun of sharing as we get older. When we're kids we share our toys (or at least we're supposed to). But as we grow up, we start sharing less. I realize I've become more possessive of my space and of my things. One thing I really like about the backpacking is the culture of sharing. When I was on Tioman, most of Melanie's things seemed to have been passed down to her by other travelers. As I continue my journey, I've been given more from other travelers, and have passed along some of my things. A guy named Blank gave me my first item at the meditation retreat - a t-shirt with the Om symbol on it. At my hostel in George Town, a Dutch guy gave me a book called "the curious incident of the dog in the night-time." It's a book written from the perspecive/writing-style of an autistic person. It was a quick read and I really enjoyed the different perspective.



The first things I've started passing along are the books that I've finished reading. The book they gave me during the Vipassana retreat. The Little Prince. Yes, they were definitely books I would have liked to keep and hold onto. But after reading through them, it only seemed fair to let others enjoy them on their trips also. Besides the hours spent together on the road or hanging out and chatting, sharing books is another form of idea sharing, which is a really important part of backpacking. Ideas of a place and culture are carried by one backpacker, interpreted through their own culture, and shared in a different time, different place, with a different group. And thankfully, idea sharing isn't another type of sharing that's been lost over time. With the internet, sharing things has become incredibly fast and easy. It's now the filtering that seems to matter more.

Second thing, partially he's had to work due to circumstance (most of us have to also). But the jobs he does, he does because of what's available, and because of how they help him in the short term. If I think of what's best for him in the long-term, it's not driving a cab 21 hours a day. Compared to him I'm lucky. But then I think, he probably does what he does because he can't imagine a better way of life, a better way to go about it and invest in the future. Although relative to him, I can look more to the future, I also imagine that there are many people out there who look at my life the way I look at this man's life. To them I look like I'm focused on the day- to-day, looking to make a quick buck and not thinking long-term. I'm striving to do that more.

Time

Time while long-term traveling is interesting. There's a documentary called "A Map For Saturday," so named because every day feels like a Saturday. It's a pretty good description. Tokyo!

Got in to Tokyo and wandered around with my mom. Feels natural, same same, just new city to explore. Very cool. In some ways very different from backpacking around. But it's cool and similar in other ways. After exploring during the day we'll just hang out and chat in the room for a few hours. Outside of the everyday routine, there's more time to just hang out. Also, I realize I'm more emotional and have less control over some strong emotions than I had thought. Darn. Oh well, just gotta rock it. Day Two. Man, Tokyo is super different, and I'm just starting to realize it. I think real culture shock usually takes some extended time living in a place to kick in, but I feel like I'm getting a small dose of it already. In the background, there's a sense of being slightly overwhelmed. A gnawing discomfort, not fundamentally "getting" things. On the surface things seem pretty similar. Big city. Shiny new buildings, good public transport, polite people dressed in business clothes. But it's like that daft punk video. Just the outside appearance is something I know. Then you notice the anime everywhere. And girls dressed up in anime costumes advertising cafes. And then you go in a cafe, and it's like you're in some alternate world where the waitstaff are roleplaying anime characters. Slightly concerning when they're all wearing schoolgirl anime outfits, customers are almost all dudes, and you're with your mom. But then, more than anything sexual, it's an alternate reality where your meal is interrupted by impromptu light shows while the girls sing songs from anime movies. Very surreal. And mixed in with this oddly captivating, childlike, internet-meme world with anime and collectibles, are high tech electronics. I played around with the Occulus Rift. I found temperature-controlled shoes. I think the US has been good with web/software startups, but we could learn from Japan just based on the hardware I've seen. Maybe I haven't looked hard enough at US hardware.
